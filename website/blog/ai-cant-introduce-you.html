AI-Assisted Memory

AI can't introduce you. But it can remember everyone you met.

trce · 2026-07-05 · 5 min read

You walked into the Token2049 side event with a single goal: meet the founder you'd been DMing for three weeks. By the end of the night you met her, plus 22 other people who all matter. By the time the cab hit the highway, you'd already mentally lost half of them.

This is the part nobody talks about at crypto conferences. Not the handshake, not the pitch, not the QR swap — the part three weeks later when you type a name into Telegram and think: who is this, again?

The bottleneck has moved

Five years ago, the hard part of conference networking was meeting people. The room was small, the introductions were formal, and you'd be lucky to leave with five cards.

Today the bottleneck is the opposite. The room has 500 people, Telegram QRs swap in three seconds, and you leave with 50 new contacts before lunch. The MIT/InsideSales lead-response research shows that the value of new contacts decays fast — reply rates collapse by 80% after the first hour. The same pattern holds for the contact itself: if you don't anchor the relationship within hours, the person blurs into a name in a list.

AI can't shake a hand for you. It can't make you charming, read the room, or remember to ask about their kid. What it can do is hold every name, face, and conversation so your brain doesn't have to.

Ebbinghaus put the forgetting curve at roughly 70% within 24 hours for new unstructured information. A conversation at a conference is about as unstructured as information gets. You heard a name, a job title, three sentences, and a few drinks' worth of context. Of course you forget by Friday.

What memory looks like in practice

The fix isn't trying harder to remember. The fix is outsourcing the memory and keeping the social part for yourself.

A real system for a high-volume crypto event looks like this:

  • Capture in under 5 seconds. Forward the Telegram QR or paper card photo to a bot. Don't open an app, don't type a name. Three taps, done.
  • Tag the moment. One word on where you met: TOKEN2049, AFTERPARTY-ZUG, SEQUENCER-PANEL. Enough to recall the context later.
  • Anchor on one claim. Before the conversation ends, type one sentence about what they said. Not their title. Their actual opinion.
  • Search, don't scroll. Three weeks later, you don't need a list — you need a question. "Who at Token2049 was working on intent-based DEXs?" Type it, get three names.

That last step is where the value compounds. A list of 200 names is a graveyard. A searchable database of 200 conversations is a Rolodex that pays for itself every quarter.

Why chat-native beats CRM-native

The traditional answer to "how do you remember everyone" was a CRM. Salesforce, HubSpot, Notion, Airtable. HubSpot's own sales research has found that 40% of salespeople say their CRM is "clunky" and a third admit they don't log half their contacts because the friction is too high.

The friction is the entire problem. If logging a contact takes 30 seconds, you stop after the 10th person of the night. If it takes 5 seconds and lives where you already are — Telegram — you log all 50.

This is why @trceiobot lives where you already do. The bot doesn't replace the conversation. It replaces the part of your brain that forgot 22 out of 23 names by Saturday.

The split that actually matters

Here's the mental model worth keeping: AI is good at memory, bad at social. You are the opposite. Stop trying to be good at both.

Lean on yourself for the part that makes you memorable — the handshake, the question, the follow-up message that references a specific thing they said. Lean on a tool for the part that makes you reliable three weeks later — knowing who they are, what they do, why you wanted to talk to them.

The intro is yours. The index is theirs.
