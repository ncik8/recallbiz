The three-line contact note that survives a conference

2026-07-23 · 4 min read

You walked into a side room at an AI summit with 14 new contacts and a phone full of half-finished notes. By the time the next panel starts, you have written “great conversation” six times and lost the one detail that would let you find any of them later.

The problem is not that you failed to document the event. You documented too much of the wrong thing. A long summary is hard to write in the moment, hard to search later, and rarely gives your future self a reason to reopen the thread.

The event is louder than your note

Microsoft’s 2025 Work Trend Index, based on aggregated Microsoft 365 activity through February 15, 2025, found that the top 20% of users by ping volume were interrupted every two minutes during core work hours. Count the full day and that becomes 275 interruptions. Nearly half of employees, 48%, said their work felt chaotic and fragmented; among leaders, the figure was 52%.

At a conference, the pings are handshakes, badge scans, room changes, group chats, and someone waving you toward the next side event. Your brain is trying to preserve context while being asked to switch contexts constantly. A blank notes app becomes a graveyard because the record exists, but the retrieval path does not.

The best contact note is not a transcript. It is a handle your future self can grab.

Three lines beat a perfect record

Use a minimum viable note. Capture only the three things that make the conversation recognisable and the next message possible:

  • Context: where you met and why the conversation happened.
  • Signal: the opinion, question, or detail that was specific to them.
  • Next move: the object, introduction, or question that gives you a reason to write.

A useful note might look like Token2049 / thinks stablecoin yield is mispriced / send APAC market map. It is not elegant. It is useful. The first line tells you where to look, the second restores the conversation, and the third prevents your follow-up from becoming a vague “checking in.”

Three lines also create a limit. When you know the note must fit in a few seconds, you stop trying to preserve every sentence and start selecting the one detail that carries the relationship.

Write for retrieval, not record-keeping

Salesforce’s 2026 State of Sales report says 51% of sales leaders identify tech silos as a barrier to their efforts. A note trapped in a separate CRM can become another silo if you never return to it. The best contact system is close to the conversation and close to the next action.

Write with the words you would actually search. A company and job title are filters. A claim, an object, or a promised action is a handle. “Talked about product” disappears. “Asked for the Asia launch timeline” gives you a search term and a reason to send the first sentence.

Capture before the context changes

Use a twenty-second loop: forward the contact to @trceiobot, add the three lines, and return to the room. If the contact arrives as a QR, a paper card, or a handle, the action is the same. The point is not to turn every meeting into admin. It is to preserve one searchable cue before the next interruption erases it.

At the end of the day, search by event and signal, not just by name. If the note answers who, why, and next, you can draft a specific opening without inventing a memory that is already gone.

Ask one question before you close the note: could you recognise this person from these three lines in three weeks? If not, replace an adjective with a fact, a role with a claim, and a summary with the next move.

Keep the part you’ll need later.
