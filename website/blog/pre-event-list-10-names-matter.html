The 10 names on your pre-event list matter more than the 200 you meet there

2026-07-13 · 5 min read

You flew to Dubai for Token2049. Three days, 200 handshakes, 80 Telegram QRs in your phone. By the cab home, half those names are already fuzzy. By next quarter, you'll close deals with maybe 4 of them. Four out of two hundred.

The volume game at crypto conferences feels productive. It isn't. The founders who actually turn events into revenue do something different the week before. They write a 10-name list. They research every name on it. And by the time the doors open, the event is a 10-meeting meeting, not a 200-coin-flip meeting.

The math nobody does

Reply-rate research across B2B sales motions has been consistent for a decade. Targeted, warm outreach converts at roughly 3x the rate of broad cold outreach — often higher at the top of the funnel. Bain's long-running B2B Pulse work keeps showing that on average, the most profitable 20% of customers generate more than 100% of a company's profit. The events version of this is brutal: the top 10% of people you meet at any given conference likely produce more than 90% of the deals you'll ever close from it.

Read that again. The 10% rule applies to who you meet, not how many you meet. Volume isn't the limiting reagent. Fit is.

Stop treating the event as a lottery. Treat it as a 10-person warm meeting where the venue happens to have 5,000 extra strangers you can ignore.

The pre-event ritual

Twelve working days before every major crypto conference we run the same ritual. It's a 30-minute commitment, and it has produced more closed deals than the next ten "networking tips" put together.

  • Speaker list → shortlist. Open the agenda. Mark every panelist, every fireside, every demo presenter. Cross-check against your CRM. Anyone who's relevant to your current pipeline gets marked. Anyone relevant to a pipeline you want gets marked. Filter that to the top 10.
  • Sponsor list → sponsor priorities. Walk the sponsor floor names. The tier-1 sponsors usually run side events, dinner parties, and demo nights — those are where the meaningful meetings happen. Pick the 5 sponsors you'd most like to talk to.
  • Backward search. For every name on your 10-list, open Telegram, X, LinkedIn. Skim their last 30 days of posts. Pull out one specific recent thing — a launch, a contrarian take, a thread they wrote. You're not stalking. You're anchoring.
  • One sentence per name. For each of the 10, write a single line you'll open with: "I saw your thread on X — curious how that lands at scale." Not a pitch. A specific observation.

That's the whole ritual. Half an hour. By the time you land in the host city, you have a meeting list, an opener for every name on it, and the social proof that you bothered to do the work nobody else does.

The opener is the moat

At a 500-person crypto event, the average introduction starts with "Hi, what do you do?" It gets the same average answer. The contact dies inside 30 seconds because nothing differentiated you from the other nine people who asked the same question in the last hour.

The opener you wrote during pre-event research — specific, recent, mildly opinionated — kills that death spiral. It's not a pitch. It's not a flex. It's the single strongest memory-encoding trick we know: semantic processing, applied to your own presence rather than theirs.

Three weeks later, when they're sorting through 300 conference follow-ups, yours is the one anchored to a thing they actually said. That's the whole competitive advantage at high-volume events. Not charm. Not volume. Specificity.

After the event

The 10-name list doesn't just shape the event. It shapes the follow-up. You already have a one-sentence recap ready for each meeting. You already have the anchor from the opener. The hand-typed follow-up that usually takes 90 minutes now takes 12.

It's also where the database starts earning its keep. The 10 warm names go in tagged. The 190 handshakes go in untagged, lower priority. Your follow-up queue knows the difference.
