The paper business card died at ETHDenver. Nobody sent flowers.

trce team · 2026-06-26 · 5 min read

You walked into the ETHDenver afterparty with a stack of 200 Moo cards in your pocket. By midnight you'd handed out six and watched 40 people scan each other's Telegram QR codes instead. The cards went home with you, unused, and stayed in your bag until you threw them out at the airport.

Paper is dead at crypto events. Not dying, not transitioning — dead. The faster you accept it, the more contacts you actually keep.

The numbers already changed

Globally, around 84% of mobile users have scanned a QR code at least once, and roughly half scan one every week. In the US, QR scans hit 89 million smartphone users in 2022 and are projected to clear 100 million by 2025 — a 26% jump in three years. The "QR is for restaurant menus" crowd is wrong. QR is the contact card now.

Telegram sits behind most of those crypto events because the numbers are unmatchable: 1 billion monthly active users as of March 2025, with about 500 million logging in daily, and roughly 2.5 million new accounts created every single day. Nobody you meet at Token2049 is unreachable on Telegram. Nobody.

The business card wasn't killed by digital. It was killed by chat — by the fact that the place where you already talk to people became the place where you also hand them your identity.

Why paper fails in crypto specifically

Three reasons paper never made sense at crypto conferences, and they keep getting worse.

  • No native handoff. A paper card is a screenshot waiting to happen — and screenshots die in camera rolls. A Telegram QR is one tap, and the contact is already in the chat app they use all day.
  • No group context. A printed card says "I'm a BD lead at a DEX." A Telegram handoff gives you a username, an avatar, a mutual groups list, and a chat you can actually open later.
  • No compliance angle. When KYC or counterparty questions come up three months later, a paper card gives you nothing. A Telegram handle gives you a starting point to verify, message, and continue the thread.

Paper has one job — get the name and number into your phone. Telegram does that job in 5 seconds, with the conversation already attached.

The 5-second capture loop

What replaced the card isn't a fancy NFC tap or an app download. It's the most boring workflow in the world: you meet someone, you scan their QR, you forward the image to @trceiobot, you keep talking.

Three taps. Zero typing. By the time you've shaken hands and said "let's chat after the panel," the contact is already in your database with their handle, their display name, and whatever event context you tagged. The handoff is invisible to the other person — they think you just took a photo.

This is why Telegram beats both the paper card and the standalone "digital card" app. The card apps require the other person to also install something. Telegram is already on their phone. The only question is whether you forward the QR or save a Moo card you'll forget in a drawer.

What to do at your next event

Leave the cards at home. Set up your Telegram QR as your lock-screen QR if you have to — make it the default way people reach you. When someone hands you their QR, forward it immediately. Don't promise to "add you on Telegram later." Forward. Done.

By the end of the event you'll have 80–250 searchable contacts instead of 12 lost paper rectangles. Three weeks later you'll remember everyone.
