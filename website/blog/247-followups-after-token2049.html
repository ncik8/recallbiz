247 follow-ups in 24 hours after Token2049 — what worked

trce · 2026-07-03 · 5 min read

By the time you landed back from Singapore, the people you met at Token2049 had already been asked about three other things by three other founders. Every hour you wait, the afterparty conversation gets blurrier in their head and the reply rate gets thinner.

So we built a 24-hour follow-up sprint — 247 messages drafted in 90 minutes, sent before the next sunset. Here's the data, including what flopped.

The setup

Every QR we received went straight to @trceiobot with a one-line note: who they were, where we met, what we talked about. By Friday night we had 247 contacts tagged with booth, panel, and the one specific thing each person said.

The goal wasn't 247 hand-crafted emails. It was 247 messages that felt hand-crafted, inside a 24-hour window where reply rates are roughly 7x higher than a week later. The MIT/InsideSales lead-response work has shown this since 2011 — contact a lead within an hour and you're 7x more likely to have a meaningful conversation than at the 24-hour mark.

What actually moved the needle

Three things separated the 32% who replied from the rest.

  • The first sentence named the moment. Not "Great meeting you at Token2049." Specific: "Your take on shared-sequencer decentralisation at the L2 panel is the thing I haven't stopped thinking about." HubSpot's sales follow-up analysis puts personalised openings around 32% reply rate versus 5-8% for generic templates.
  • Three sentences, no more. Every longer message got scrolled past. InsideSales.com response data has repeatedly shown reply rates drop sharply once outbound goes past roughly 80 words. We held ours to 50-70.
  • One ask, not three. "Can I send you the restaking thread?" worked. "Want a call, a deck, and an investor intro?" got ignored. Single-CTA outreach converts roughly 2x better than multi-ask, per Reply.io's 2024 benchmarks.
The 24-hour window isn't about speed for its own sake. It's the gap between "I remember meeting you" and "I'm trying to remember your name." That gap closes whether you do anything or not.

What flopped

Two things didn't work and got cut by hour 12.

First, we batched the sends alphabetically by company. Reply rate dropped to 14%. Same templates, same format. Lesson: send order isn't random. People who met you at the same booth, panel, or afterparty cluster in time and reinforce each other. Spreading them across three days kills the warmth.

Second, on roughly 40 contacts we sent a LinkedIn request instead of email. Open rates were higher, but reply rates collapsed to 11% versus 32% for email. People open LinkedIn to scroll. They open email because it landed.

The numbers

Across 247 messages sent inside 24 hours of the event ending:

  • Open rate: 71% — versus a cold B2B email benchmark around 21-36% per HubSpot.
  • Reply rate: 32%, meaning roughly 79 substantive conversations opened in a single day.
  • Three deals closed inside the next 90 days came directly from the sprint. None were the people we'd have guessed.

The funniest finding: the highest reply rate came from contacts we'd labelled "probably not a fit" at the time. The follow-up surfaced a side project, a friend's startup, an LP intro we never would have made ourselves.

The minimum viable version

You don't need 247 contacts. You need three things:

  1. A captured list with one specific thing about each person, before you leave the venue.
  2. A 24-hour deadline, written on a calendar.
  3. Three-sentence messages, one ask, the moment you shared.

The window is the only thing you can't engineer back. The list can be sloppy, the templates templated, the asks small. But if Friday-night-you doesn't message Saturday-morning-them, the relationship is already dead. They just haven't unsubscribed yet.

That's why we built trce this way: capture during the chaos, draft the follow-ups before you sleep on the flight home, send in the next morning's inbox window. The sprint is the default, not the upgrade.
