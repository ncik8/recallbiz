Memory

You forgot their name by Tuesday. Here's the retrieval anchor that fixes it.

trce · 2026-07-14 · 5 min read

You walked out of Token2049 with 247 Telegram QRs in your phone. By Tuesday morning you scrolled through the list, saw a name, and realised you had no idea who that person was. By Friday you had stopped opening the app.

The curve is brutal and predictable

Ebbinghaus published the forgetting curve in 1885. A century later, the data has barely moved: without reinforcement, you forget roughly 70% of new information within 24 hours and 90% within a week. His own experiments put 24-hour recall at around 30-40% of what you took in. The curve has been replicated on names, faces, and conversations since.

Crypto conferences make the curve steeper. After 200 introductions in 48 hours, the brain is not just tired — it is actively discarding. Working memory holds about four items at once. By the time you meet the seventh person at a booth, the first is already gone.

The capture habit is solved. The retention habit is the new bottleneck. Most contact databases are tombs by Wednesday.

The one-line note

Memory researchers call this the encoding specificity principle: you remember something better when the cue at retrieval matches the cue at encoding. In plain language: write down one specific thing at the moment you meet someone, and that one thing is the handle you grab onto later.

It does not have to be useful. It does not have to be flattering. It has to be specific enough that the next time you see their name, the conversation comes back in colour.

Examples from a real Token2049 week:

  • "Kept using the word 'front-running' as a compliment."
  • Launched a zkVM joke I still think about.
  • Disagreed with me about L2 sequencers — won the argument.
  • Wore the bright green jacket, works on intent-based DEXes.
  • Asked me a question I couldn't answer. I owe them one.

None of these are roles. None are "BD at X protocol". They are anchors — strange, specific, retrieval-grade. Three weeks later, when their name pops up, the anchor fires and the whole conversation returns.

Why most contact databases fail

The standard fields — name, company, role, Telegram handle — give you zero retrieval power. A list of "BD, BD, BD, founder, founder, eng" tells you nothing about who you actually liked talking to.

This is why so many founders abandon their CRM by week three. The data is correct and useless. You open it, scroll, see no faces, close it. A year later you remember the jacket person and cannot find them because you tagged them as "BD at Unknown Protocol".

The discipline that changes this:

  • Capture the QR within 5 seconds, while you are still standing next to them.
  • Voice-note a 10-second anchor right after: "Wears glasses, disagreed about restaking, asked me about my airdrop thesis."
  • Forward both to @trceiobot with one tap. The note becomes searchable text. The QR becomes the contact.
  • Open the database on day 4 — Wednesday, not Monday. Monday the context is still fresh. Wednesday it isn't. If you can still place every face, your system works.

That Wednesday test is the one that matters. If your database is intelligible three days after the event, you will actually use it.

The compound effect

One anchor per person, applied to 200 people, builds something most founders never have: a database you can search by meaning. Type "the one who disagreed about restaking" three weeks later and the name pops up. The search works because the encoding was specific, not because the tool was smart.

Traditional CRMs were built for sales teams meeting five leads a week. They model the relationship as a pipeline. Conference attendees meet 200 people in a weekend. The pipeline model collapses. The memory model wins.

Capture is solved. Retrieval is the frontier. Write one weird, specific, true thing while it is still fresh, and you will recognise them a month later.
