Conference tactics

The 48 hours before a conference matter more than the 48 after

trce · 2026-07-07 · 5 min read

You just landed for Token2049. Your inbox has 240 unread messages. The first afterparty starts in three hours. By the time you reach the hotel, you've already lost the war — because the war was won or lost on Tuesday, when you could have spent two hours on the attendee list and didn't.

Everyone obsesses over the post-event follow-up window. That window is real. It's just not the highest-leverage two hours of the entire conference.

The pre-event 2-hour investment

Open the attendee list two days before you fly. Pull 10 names. Not 50. Ten. The people whose work you've actually read — a thread they wrote, a research post, a protocol they shipped against, a fund they raised from. Read their last three public posts. Note one specific thing you can ask them about that isn't "what do you do."

This is the step that separates the people who collect 240 QRs from the people who collect 10 conversations that actually go somewhere. Bain's B2B selling research has shown for years that sellers who personalise the first message see roughly 3x higher reply rates than those running generic templates — and the personalisation only works if you did the reading before the room.

Pre-event research isn't optional. It's the entire reason the conversation has any warmth at all.

What to look up

You don't need a full dossier. You need four things per name, and they take roughly 4 minutes each to gather:

  • Their last public take. A thread, a podcast clip, a research post. The thing they were visibly proud of or visibly wrong about.
  • One sharp question. Not "tell me about your work." Something specific enough that it can't be answered with the elevator pitch.
  • What they're not. Most attendees at a crypto event are either a builder, an investor, an operator, or press. Knowing which they aren't saves you from pitching to a journalist or pitching an investor on a use case they've already heard twice that day.
  • One shared reference point. A panel they're speaking on, a paper they cited, a fund they backed, a contrarian bet they took.

Four minutes each. Ten names. Forty minutes of reading. The remaining eighty minutes is for the booth map and the schedule — knowing which rooms to be in at which hour.

The follow-up window is a function of the introduction. A warm intro multiplies reply rates by 3x. A cold one wastes the only window you had.

The booth-map hour

The second hour is logistics. Print the booth map. Mark the four booths you'll actually go to. Mark the two panels you won't leave early. Mark the one afterparty you're going to even though it's inconvenient.

McKinsey's 2023 B2B pulse found that the average "high-volume conference attendee" wanders between 60-80% of their floor time — meaning most of the hours you spent at the event were hours you spent walking between unrelated conversations. A printed map with 7 marks cuts the wandering to under 30% and reclaims roughly 90 minutes a day for actual conversation.

The wandering is the silent killer. You tell yourself you're "open to serendipity." What you're actually doing is bumping into the same six people who also weren't being strategic about the room.

What changes in the room

Here's the part nobody talks about. When you walk up to someone you've already read, the entire interaction collapses into the actual conversation in under 15 seconds. No role recital. No elevator pitch. No "so what do you do" loop that takes 90 seconds to confirm whether the other person is even relevant.

The 30-second introduction that you've rehearsed in the mirror is suddenly irrelevant — because you've already skipped past the parts that usually eat the 30 seconds. You walk up, you name the thing you read, you ask the sharp question. The conversation starts where it would normally have ended.

Three weeks later, when you're looking at your contact database trying to remember who to follow up with, those are the people you'll remember. Not because they handed you a paper card with a fancy logo. Because the conversation itself was a conversation, not a handshake.

The pre-event checklist

Two days before the next event you fly to, do this:

  1. Pull 10 names from the attendee list. Read their last 3 public posts.
  2. Write 10 sharp questions — one per name, specific enough to break the elevator pitch.
  3. Print the booth map. Mark 4 booths, 2 panels, 1 afterparty. Stick to it.
  4. Open @trceiobot in Telegram and type /trip set {EventName} so the bot knows what trip to attribute incoming QRs to.
  5. Forward yourself the four-minute scan as a single message. Read it on the plane. Refresh the names in your head before you land.

Total time: 100 minutes. Return on the investment: roughly the difference between 240 cold QRs and 240 conversations that turn into the kind of follow-up that doesn't feel like work.

The post-event 48 hours matter. They still matter. But they're downstream of decisions made on Tuesday — and most people skip Tuesday entirely.
