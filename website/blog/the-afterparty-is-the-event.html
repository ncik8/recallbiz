Conference tactics

The afterparty is the event. The panel is the warmup.

trce · 2026-06-28 · 6 min read

You sat through the panel on restaking economics for 45 minutes. By 11pm at the rooftop bar three blocks away, you'd met two GPs, a founder raising his seed, and the protocol engineer who'd actually been shipping the spec. The panel was the warmup. The bar was the event.

You keep optimising for the wrong hour. At a 2,000-person crypto summit, the main stage is content you could watch on YouTube the next morning. What you flew in for happens between 9pm and 1am, off the official schedule, often off the official venue.

The math nobody admits

Eventbrite's industry research puts the average conference attendee at roughly 8–12 meaningful conversations per day. Bain's B2B events survey lands in a similar range. That sounds healthy until you split it by setting: most studies tracking conference behaviour find the bulk of those high-quality interactions happen in sponsored side events, dinners, and lounges, not in the keynote hall. HubSpot's 2024 State of Marketing report logged a related pattern: 75% of B2B buyers say the relationships that close deals get built in informal settings, not formal demos. The keynote theatre is a very expensive pre-game.

The panel exists for two reasons. To give the speakers a credential. And to give the afterparty something to talk about.

The panel exists to give the afterparty something to talk about.

What "the afterparty" actually is in 2026

It's not the dance-floor-branded thing in the press release. It's smaller, messier, and almost always Telegram-coordinated. A 30-person dinner at a Saigon pho place. A 12-person rooftop with a Protocol Labs sticker on the door. A 6-person walk to a kebab shop after the closing drinks.

Granovetter's 1973 weak-ties research — still the most cited finding in network science — showed that the acquaintances you barely remember are the source of 56% of new jobs, partnerships, and high-value introductions. Not your work best friend. Not the speaker you waited in line to meet. The stranger you sat next to at a 14-person dinner.

Crypto events run on this pattern even more than legacy B2B. The whole point of flying to TOKEN2049 or KBW or Devcon is to compress 200 weak-tie collisions into three days. Most of them happen between 9pm and 1am.

The system for showing up

Treat the daytime programme as a content channel, not a networking channel. Then build a real schedule for the night.

  • Pick three dinners or side events in advance, not after you land. The good ones fill up two weeks out.
  • Arrive 20 minutes late to panels. Sit in the back. Leave 15 minutes early. The hallway track is where weak ties form.
  • Find the Telegram group for the side event you want, not the main conference one. The real logistics live in smaller chats.
  • Save contacts at the afterparty, not the panel. The person you met over whiskey at 11pm is the one you'll forget by Tuesday — anchor them on what you disagreed about.
  • Pre-book nothing for the morning after. Sleep in. The strongest follow-ups get drafted between 10am and noon, not at 2am in the lobby.

The capture step is the part that collapses. By the third afterparty of the night, opening an app to type a name feels like homework. That's why the entire contact system has to live in a place you're already looking at — your Telegram, where the conversation happened. Forward the QR while you're still standing at the bar. Three taps. Done before the next round.

Reframe the week

Stop booking the conference. Book the after-parties. Pick the city for the dinners you can get into, the side events you can squeeze into, the 11pm conversations that aren't on any agenda. The panel is a tax you pay to earn the right to be in the room at midnight.

Everything else is just warmup.
