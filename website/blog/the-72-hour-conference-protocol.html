Conference Tactics

The 72-hour conference protocol: before, during, and after.

trce team · 2026-06-30 · 5 min read

You land in Singapore on a Tuesday night. Token2049 opens Wednesday morning and runs through Thursday. By Friday morning, you've collected 200 Telegram QRs, promised 60 intros, and forgotten 40 names. By Monday, you've followed up with 12. By next month, you've followed up with 0.

The event itself isn't the bottleneck. The 72 hours around the event are. Here's the protocol that survives contact volumes most networking advice never accounts for.

The day before: pick 10

Most people walk into a 5,000-person conference and try to meet everyone. They meet no one. Bain & Company's research on B2B events keeps reaching the same conclusion: the highest-ROI attendees arrive with a short, pre-validated target list, not an open calendar.

Before any conference, do this:

  • Pull the speaker and sponsor lists. Star the 10 names that map to your next quarter's pipeline.
  • Send each one a single Telegram message, two days out. Reference something they wrote or shipped — not "would love to connect."
  • Book the two highest-value meetings before you fly. Everything else is opportunistic.
  • Open @trceiobot, type /trip set {conference name}, and tag the people you actually want to find again. The bot will surface them in any search later.

That's the whole pre-event system. It takes 90 minutes. It saves 30 hours of dead-end conversations.

The day of: capture, don't converse

Once the event starts, your job is not to have great conversations. Your job is to have captured great conversations. The conversations themselves will be forgotten. The database won't.

The 247 contacts you collect at a 3-day event are worthless if you can't find the 4 that matter three weeks from now.

The capture loop has to take less than 5 seconds. If it takes 30 seconds, you stop doing it after the 12th person, and the rest of the event evaporates. That's why the only viable primitive is forward — open Telegram, forward the QR, done. Anything that asks you to type a name or open a separate app collapses at high volume.

A few specific moves:

  • Tag the moment in the conversation when you forward the QR, not later. "The one who thinks the next cycle is RWAFi" beats "the one from the panel."
  • Voice notes beat typed notes. A 6-second clip gets tone, intent, and the joke. None of that survives a typed summary.
  • Forward screenshots of the group chats, the TG channel invites, the X DMs. Your future self doesn't remember the channel name.

The day after: the 90-minute follow-up window

This is the part everyone skips. InsideSales.com's Lead Response Management study found that the odds of contacting a lead drop 100x in the first hour, and the response rate on a 30-minute-old lead is 21x higher than on a 30-day-old one. The same curve applies to conference contacts — except you collected 200 of them on the same day, and the curve is steeper.

By hour 24 after the conference ends, your reply rate is somewhere between 40% and 60%. By hour 72, it's 10% to 15%. By the following Monday, you're back to 5% and you're competing with 200 other people who met the same person at the same booth.

So the morning after the closing party, before you fly home, you spend 90 minutes on this:

  • Open the trip in @trceiobot. Ask for a follow-up draft per contact.
  • Each draft is 3 sentences, references one thing you actually said, and proposes one next step.
  • You skim, edit the 10% that need a personal touch, and send the rest.

240 follow-ups in 90 minutes. Not 240 generic "great to meet you" lines — 240 messages tied to a specific moment in a specific conversation.

Why this beats the contact app

Most CRMs and contact apps fail at conferences because they assume you'll sit down afterward and type things in. You won't. The 5-second forward loop is the only capture primitive that survives a 12-hour day on your feet.

The rest of the system — the 10-name pre-list, the 5-second capture, the 90-minute follow-up window — is just structure around that single primitive. Skip any of the three and the database degrades. Keep all three and you walk out of Token2049 with a relationship list, not a stack of paper cards.
