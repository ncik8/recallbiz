Networking psychology

The conversation topic that gets you remembered three weeks on

trce · 2026-06-28 · 5 min read

You walked into the afterparty at Devcon. By midnight you had twelve business cards and three Telegram QRs. By the following Monday you could remember two names, one job title, and one opinion. Three weeks later, only the opinion was still there.

That last part isn't a coincidence. It's how memory works — and it's why most networking advice is solving the wrong problem.

Job titles are write-only memory

When someone tells you "I'm a growth lead at a Layer 2," your brain files the words in the slowest part of working memory and moves on. By the third introduction of the night, the file cabinet is full. Job titles and company names are write-only — they go in, but they don't survive retrieval three weeks later.

What does survive is anything emotionally charged. A take you disagreed with. A prediction you found bold. A question that was unusually specific. Stanford's Mark Granovetter showed back in 1973 that the connections carrying actual new information — the "weak ties" — are the ones that change your trajectory, not the title-and-handshake kind.

People don't remember what you do. They remember what you said, and whether they agreed with it.

The one question that does the work

Skip "what do you do?" It generates a job title and a polite nod. Instead, ask one of these — they all force the other person to commit to a position, which is what your memory actually hooks onto:

  • "What's a consensus take in your space that you think is wrong?"
  • "What did the panel get wrong today?"
  • "If you had to bet on which of these three narratives wins in 2027, which?"
  • "What's the most overhyped thing in your stack right now?"

The first one is the strongest. It works at every crypto event, every founder dinner, every AI meetup. It takes 5 seconds to ask and almost always produces a 90-second answer. By the time you walk away, you have a hook: this is the person who thinks ZK rollups are overhyped. That single sentence is enough to reconstruct the whole conversation a month later.

Save the take, not the title

The memory hook only works if you write it down. That's the second half of the system. When you forward a contact to @trceiobot, the field worth filling in isn't "Head of BD at SequencerCo" — it's the line that would let you reconstruct the conversation. A few examples worth saving:

  • "thinks modular L1s are a 2024 narrative, already priced in"
  • "disagreed with the restaking pitch — said it collapses the security model"
  • "asked me about sequencer decentralisation, building on a Celestia fork"
  • "predicted the next bull is driven by real-world assets, not L2s"

Two months later, when you open your database and search for "overhyped" or "ZK," that person comes back. You can write a follow-up that references the actual conversation: "You said X — has anything changed your view?" Reply rates on that kind of message are dramatically higher than the generic "great meeting you at TOKEN2049" follow-up most people send.

The system

Three habits, total:

  • Ask one opinion-anchoring question in the first 30 seconds of every introduction.
  • Forward the QR to @trceiobot within 5 seconds, with the take written down.
  • Search your database by opinion, not by name, when you follow up.

That's the whole system. The trick isn't being more memorable. It's anchoring on the only part of the conversation that survives encoding — and writing it down in the only field that survives retrieval.

Try it at the next event you go to.
