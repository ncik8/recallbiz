Networking

The 6 strangers at conferences who actually matter

2026-07-15 · 6 min read

You walked into Token2049 and ran into three people from your own fund, one from your previous startup, and the founder you've been DMing for six months. Easy conversations. Warm handshakes. You feel like you had a great event.

You didn't. The deal that closes six months from now will come from the stranger you met for 90 seconds at the afterparty — the one you almost didn't save.

The 1973 study

Mark Granovetter, then a Harvard grad student, asked 282 Boston professionals how they found their current job. He expected strong ties — close friends, family — to dominate. They didn't. Of the people who found work through personal contacts, 83.4% used weak ties (acquaintances, people they saw occasionally), while only 17% leaned on close friends and family. The paper, The Strength of Weak Ties, quietly rewrote how we think about networks.

Ronald Burt extended the work decades later with structural holes: the most valuable people in your network are the ones who bridge disconnected groups. A founder who knows ten other founders isn't useful to you. A founder who knows ten founders and ten regulators and ten LPs you don't know is a goldmine.

Strong ties already know what you know. Weak ties are the only place new information can enter your world.

This is the part most networking advice misses. The warm intro from your friend gives you the same five intros you already had.

Why your list is wrong

Most people leave events with a contact list weighted toward people they already know. You ran into your friend from college. You met the founder you've been following on X for a year. You had coffee with the panelist who spoke at your old job.

None of those contacts are new information. They reinforce what you already have. The strangers — the awkward 90-second hand-offs at the espresso bar, the person whose badge you read in the elevator — those are the ones expanding your network's reach.

A practical split to keep in mind at any 500-person event:

  • Strong ties (20%) — people you already know well. Easy, low-value follow-up.
  • Weak ties (60%) — acquaintances, people you've met once. Where most new opportunities live.
  • Structural bridges (20%) — strangers who connect you to groups you don't already access. Highest value.

If your contact list is mostly the first bucket, you went to the event for the food.

The 6-stranger protocol

Here's the system that re-weights your list toward weak ties, without making you an obnoxious networker:

  • The elevator slot. Scan the badge of the person next to you. Ask one question. Save their Telegram QR before the doors open.
  • The food line slot. The lunch queue is the highest-density weak-tie zone in any conference.
  • The sponsor-booth skip. Talk to the people standing awkwardly next to booths holding espresso.
  • The afterparty filter. Approach the two standing alone at the bar, not the group that arrived together.
  • The 5-second save. Every weak tie lands in your searchable database within 5 seconds, not at the hotel later.

Six per day across three days is 18 new structural bridges — the number Granovetter found actually changes a network's reach.
