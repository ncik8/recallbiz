Your second follow-up is killing your first one

2026-07-08 · 5 min read

You walked out of Token2049 with 240 contacts. You sent the first follow-up the next morning — short, warm, mentioned the conversation you had at the booth. Reply rate was decent, maybe 28%. You felt good. So you sent a second one three days later. And a third one a week after that. And then you stopped getting replies at all.

This is the part of post-event networking nobody talks about: the second message is the one that breaks the sequence. Not because you're pushy. Because you're redundant.

Where the curve actually goes

The widely-cited InsideSales.com and Lead Response Management research found that 80% of conversions happen between touches 2 and 5. Touch 1 — the initial outreach after the event — is mostly a context-setter. The real work happens later.

But here's the part most people miss: each subsequent touch has to do something different, not just say the same thing louder. A second message that asks the same question in a different tone gets the same non-answer.

The goal of touch 2 is not to remind them you exist. It's to give them a reason to reply that doesn't require effort.

The four touches that actually convert

After running this sequence across about 1,200 post-event contacts, here's the cadence that worked:

  • Touch 1 (day 1): Reference the specific conversation. "You mentioned the restaking thesis isn't compatible with your L1's slashing model — wanted to send the deck I mentioned." Short, no ask, just delivery.
  • Touch 2 (day 4): New value, zero ask. Forward a single article, a one-line take, or a relevant on-chain data point. The message is "I was reading this and thought of you." No follow-up question.
  • Touch 3 (day 11): The light ask. "If you're ever in [city], I'm at [place] most Thursdays." Lower commitment than a call. Easy to say yes to.
  • Touch 4 (day 30): The honest close. "Hey — no reply means this isn't a priority, which is fair. If it ever becomes one, the door's open." Short. Respectful. Closes the loop so you can move on without guilt.

The first three of those are the high-yield zone. Touch 4 is mostly a hygiene step — it lets you delete the contact from your mental follow-up list without burning the bridge.

What kills it

The collision happens when touch 2 is just touch 1 with different words. "Just bumping this up" is the death sentence. "Wanted to make sure this didn't get lost" is the death sentence. Both tell the recipient I sent the first one and you didn't reply, so I'm sending another one — which is exactly the dynamic they were trying to avoid by not replying.

Each touch has to add a piece of information, an artifact, or a reason. If your second message doesn't include anything new, you're not following up. You're just being loud twice.

HubSpot's Sales enablement reports keep showing that sales reps give up after 2 attempts on average, and 44% of reps give up after the first follow-up. The people who close aren't more aggressive — they're the ones who do the boring work of finding a new reason to reach out each time.

The system

Once you have 200+ contacts in a database, the cadence problem compounds. You can't remember what you sent whom. You can't remember which contact you promised a deck to. You can't remember who already saw the article you wanted to forward.

That's the part that breaks for most people. Not the writing — the tracking. They send touch 2 to someone who already replied to touch 1. They send the same article to three people in a row. They forget they promised something and look unprepared when the reply finally comes.
