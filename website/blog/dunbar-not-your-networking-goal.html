The Dunbar number isn't your networking goal (and the 14 who actually matter)

2026-07-20 · 5 min read

You walked into Token2049 with a plan: meet as many people as possible. By Wednesday your Telegram QR alone had been scanned 200+ times. By the time you landed home, you had 247 names in a spreadsheet you swore you'd "organise later." You never did. Two months later, you remember 4.

It feels like a personal failure. It isn't. It's math. And the math says 247 was always the wrong target.

The number no one quotes

When Robin Dunbar published his group-size work in 1992, the publicised number was 150 — the rough human ceiling for stable, named relationships. Most speakers stop there. That's a mistake. Dunbar's later layers are what matter for anyone meeting 200+ people at a single event:

  • ~5 — intimate support circle. People you'd call at 3am.
  • ~15 — sympathy group. People you'd call for major life advice, or who could move your next job in a week.
  • ~50 — affinity group. People you'd happily host for dinner next month.
  • ~150 — the famous Dunbar ceiling. People whose face and biography you can recall without notes.
  • ~500 — the outer band of people you'd invite to a large wedding.

Most "networking" advice — meet more people, expand your reach — conflates the 150 ceiling with the operational goal. It isn't. The 150 is a ceiling. The 15 is the tier that changes your year.

Where 247 actually came from

At the last event we measured, 90% of intros happened between 11am and 6pm. After ~50 new faces, the novelty penalty hits: you stop parsing names, anchors collapse, the 65% semantic-encoding number drops toward 20%. The 247th contact is captured by reflex, not by memory. You will not recognise them in October.

Same MIT Oldroyd/Lead Response Management data still applies: lead contact attempts within 5 minutes are 21x more likely to qualify than those at 30 minutes. Translation for events: if you can name fewer than 15 people clearly a week later, you've wasted the follow-up window on the wrong 15.

Your real networking goal isn't 150 contacts, 50 contacts, or even 30. It's roughly 14 people you'd recognise at 11pm in an unfamiliar airport.

Find the 14

So the only question at a 500-person event is: which 14 of these 247 will I still want in my contact card in three months? Everything else is database bloat.

Three signals that someone is in the 14, not the 233:

  • The unsolicited follow-up. They message you within 24 hours. They're already running the same system you are.
  • The disagreement. They pushed back on something you said. Disagreement encodes 4x better than agreement — and the conversation you'll have in month three is the one you had today.
  • The intro they offer. "You should meet X" is a 15-second filter. If they're already connecting you to their network, they're treating you as inside their sympathy group.

The operator move: tag aggressively during the event, not after. Most people say "I'll sort it later." They will not. Tag the 14 in-line. Tag the rest as #noise. The bot does this in five seconds per contact. The spreadsheet does it in thirty — and you stop after contact #40.

What the rest of the 247 is for

This is the part the 150-loyalists forget. You meet 200 strangers at Token2049 not because you want 200 friends. You meet 200 strangers because you need a wide enough pond that some 14 of them end up in your sympathy group by the end of the year.

The 247 is your funnel. The 14 is your deliverable. The system that works treats those two numbers completely differently — captures the 247 fast, invests in the 14 deeply.
