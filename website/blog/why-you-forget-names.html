Why you forget 9 out of 10 names within 10 seconds (and the one hack that actually works)

2026-06-21 · 5 min read

You shook 80 hands at Consensus. By the cab ride to the airport you remember 6 names. By Monday you remember 1. It's not your memory. It's how names are encoded in your brain when you don't tie them to anything.

The standard advice — "just repeat their name back three times" — doesn't work. It never has.

The science

In 1972, two Canadian psychologists named Craik and Lockhart ran a series of experiments that quietly rewrote how we understand memory. They found that the depth at which you process information determines how well you remember it.

Process a name structurally — hearing the sound of it, the way it looks on a business card — and you remember it 17% of the time. Process it phonemically — repeating it, sounding it out — and you remember it 36% of the time. Process it semantically — tying it to a meaning, an idea, something the person said — and you remember it 65% of the time.

That's a 4x lift over "just repeat their name back." And almost nobody does the semantic version, because it requires you to be present in the conversation instead of panicking about whether you'll remember the name.

The forgetting curve

Hermann Ebbinghaus figured out in 1885 that you forget roughly 75% of new information within 24 hours. Most of that loss happens in the first hour.

So the window to "cement" a name is during the conversation itself, not later when you're back at the hotel typing it into a spreadsheet.

This is why "I'll write it down later" never works. By the time you sit down to write, the encoding didn't happen. There's nothing left to write.

The hack

Anchor on one specific thing they said. Not their company. Not their role. Something they claimed, something they asked, something they disagreed with.

The autopilot is the enemy. After 50 introductions, your brain switches off and every name goes into the same unmarked drawer.

Examples:

  • "You're the one who thinks ZK rollups are overhyped, right?"
  • "You asked me about L2 sequencer decentralisation at the panel"
  • "You disagreed with the restaking pitch — why?"

That single anchor is enough to recall the whole conversation when you see their name in your database three weeks later.

The trick is making it automatic. Pick the anchor before you introduce yourself. Watch their badge. Listen to the first sentence after "what do you do?" If they say "I work on restaking" — pick a stance. Disagree. Ask a sharp follow-up. The disagreement creates the anchor.

The system

Anchor created. Conversation had. Now what?

The 30-second introduction gets you through the meeting. The next part is making sure you can find the person again three weeks later when you want to follow up. That's where most people lose.

Paper business cards go in a drawer. Typed names go in a spreadsheet you never open. Telegram handles go in a chat list you scroll past.

The system that works: forward the contact to something that indexes it within seconds. Within 5 seconds of meeting someone, the contact should be in a database searchable by name, company, role, and — critically — the anchor you created.

Then three weeks later when you're following up, you don't have to remember. You search "ZK rollups" and the person's contact card appears with the note "thinks they're overhyped" attached. The anchor survives even when your memory doesn't.

The next event you go to, try this: at every introduction, before you say your own name, decide on one anchor. Disagree with something they said. Ask a sharp follow-up. Then forward their Telegram QR to a contact memory system within 5 seconds.

The combination — semantic encoding + immediate capture + searchable database — is the only reliable way to remember 80 people three weeks later. Memory alone won't do it. Repetition won't do it. The hack will.
