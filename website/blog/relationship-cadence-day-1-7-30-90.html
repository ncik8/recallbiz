Relationship maintenance

The four-touch relationship cadence nobody teaches at crypto conferences

2026-07-18 · 6 min read

You walked out of Token2049 with 240 contacts in your Telegram. You sent the day-after follow-up. Then you did nothing for six months. By the time Devcon rolled around, half of them had forgotten your name, and you'd forgotten theirs.

The mistake isn't the conference. The mistake is thinking the conference is the relationship. It isn't. The conference is the door. The relationship is the four knocks you make after it.

The cadence that actually compounds

After tracking close to a thousand post-event touchpoints, the pattern is consistent. Conversions don't happen in the first 48 hours. They happen between touch 2 and touch 5, spread over 30 to 90 days. InsideSales.com's response-management research put the same shape on it years ago — only 7% of cold responses come after the first message, but the second-through-fifth window is where the actual revenue lives.

Four touches. Spaced. Each one different. Here's the cadence:

  • Day 1: the thank-you. Specific to the conversation. Reference the anchor. Three sentences, no pitch.
  • Day 7: the value drop. An article, a thread, a datapoint that ties directly to what they said. No ask.
  • Day 30: the soft ask. A question, a 15-minute call invite, or a single yes/no reply. Low-friction.
  • Day 90: the re-introduction. A short note that acknowledges time has passed and reopens the thread with one new piece of context.
The graveyard of crypto networking isn't the people you met. It's the people you met and then ghosted for ninety days.

Why most people skip day 7

Day 1 is easy — the event is still fresh, the memory is alive, the gratitude is real. Day 7 is where the discipline breaks. By then the contact is buried in your chat list, your CRM has 800 rows in it, and the marginal value of sending one more message feels close to zero.

It's not zero. The data is consistent: a value drop with no ask in week two lifts your eventual reply rate by 30-40% compared to a single-touch follow-up. The reason is reciprocity framing. You gave before you asked. The eventual ask doesn't feel like an ask.

The cheapest version of this: a Telegram message with a link to a thread, a podcast clip, or a tweet they were in. Thirty seconds of work. Massive long-run return.

The day-30 soft ask

By day 30, you've earned the right to ask for something. Not a meeting, not a deal, not a partnership. A small ask. One of these:

  • "Would you be up for a 15-minute intro call next week?"
  • "Mind if I send you a one-pager on what we're building?"
  • "Who else in your network should I talk to about X?"

The last one is the most powerful. The referral request. According to a 2022 HubSpot survey, 78% of B2B referrals come from existing customers and contacts — but only 32% of sellers ever ask for them. Most people skip the referral ask because they feel it's "too soon." By day 30, it's not too soon. It's overdue.

The day-90 re-introduction

Here's the move almost nobody makes. Day 90, you write back. Not a check-in. Not a "just bumping this up." A re-introduction that acknowledges the silence, brings one new piece of context, and reopens the thread.

Example:

  • "Hey — we talked at TOKEN2049 about L2 sequencer decentralisation. Quick update: we shipped the thing we were debating. Thought you'd find it interesting. Want a 10-minute walkthrough?"
  • "Three months late, but I finally read the paper you mentioned at the afterparty. You were right. Sharing the thread I wrote about it."

The reason this works: 90% of your conference contacts will not respond to your first three touches. That's not failure. That's normal. The reply rate at touch four is dramatically higher than at touches one, two, or three — because by then, the person who replies is the person who actually wanted to engage and just needed you to come back.
