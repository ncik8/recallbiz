Networking systems

Networking is a database problem, not a personality problem

trce · 2026-07-19 · 5 min read

You walked into KBW with 247 new contacts in your Telegram. By the time you flew home, you could name 12. Three weeks later, you could find 3 when you actually needed them — and one of those was the guy you argued with about rollups.

You didn't lose the contacts. They're still on your phone, still in some app you barely open. You lost the ability to find them. That's a database problem. And no amount of charisma fixes it.

You were never going to remember 247 names

Robin Dunbar's research put the cognitive limit on stable social relationships at roughly 150. That's the people you can actually hold in your head — names, faces, context, last conversation. A four-day crypto conference can drop 300 to 500 names on you in a single week.

Your brain isn't broken. It's working exactly as designed. The names you remember are the ones tied to specific anchors — the panel question, the shared joke, the second drink. The other 400% of your list is just noise that decays within 48 hours.

Networking isn't about meeting more people. It's about being able to find the right one six weeks later when you actually need them.

The Granovetter problem

Mark Granovetter's 1973 paper "The Strength of Weak Ties" found that 83% of job placements came through acquaintances — not close friends. The people you barely remember are statistically the most valuable ones in your contact graph. They're the bridges to other rooms.

But a weak tie is only useful if you can retrieve it. And retrieval requires two things your phone doesn't give you:

  • A specific anchor: what did this person say, ask, or care about?
  • A search surface: when you need a "DEX designer in Singapore", can you find them in 20 seconds?

If the answer is scrolling through 1,400 contacts looking for an avatar, the weak tie is dead. You'll default to whoever you remember, and that's usually the loudest person in the room — not the most useful.

Retrieval beats recall

The shift that actually works at scale is treating your contact list like a database, not a memory. Three rules make this stick:

  • Capture at the moment. If saving takes more than five seconds, you stop doing it after contact 30. Forward to a bot, snap a photo, voice-memo a tag — whatever collapses the action to a single gesture.
  • Anchor with one weird thing. Not their role. Not their company. The thing they disagreed with, the question that made you think, the detail you laughed about. That anchor is your retrieval key.
  • Search, don't scroll. When you need "the person from the panel who cared about MEV", your system should answer in seconds. If you have to remember names, you've already lost.

This is why we built @trceiobot as a Telegram-native inbox. The capture is a forward. The anchor is a one-line note. The search is a Telegram message. There's no app to open, no form to fill, no CRM to learn. The friction is the thing that kills every other system.

The honest test

Six weeks from now, you won't remember the 247 people you met at the next conference. That's biology. The question is whether, when someone mentions a contact you have, you can pull up the anchor in 20 seconds and write a context-aware follow-up.

If the answer is no, you didn't network. You collected business cards.
